The sourcing

The Pittsburgh Water investigation did not run on the dataset alone. It ran on people: an environmental director, a RAND researcher, a media-law attorney, board members answering for their own record, community organizers who once watched the board argue in public, and the utility's own written responses to every question. This is the map of who said what, and which published claim each one carries.

Outside experts

Myron Arnowitt

Pennsylvania State Director, Clean Water Action

Recorded interview · November 3, 2025

"When there is political capture, unanimous votes are one of the results — the system's being run by an entity that's outside the actual agency."
"Their job is to ask questions, to make sure they understand what the utility is doing. If no one's really asking a lot of questions, then they probably don't really know what's actually happening."
Carries the story's framing of board questions as the core oversight function, the executive-session red flags, and the distinction between benign consensus and political capture.

Linnea Warren Mears

Policy researcher, RAND Corporation; author of a 2017 study of Pittsburgh Water's lead remediation options

Recorded interview · November 2025

"Meaningful participation changes a decision, or there can be a clear line drawn between what the participation has involved and what the utility has done."
"Trust is really hard to repair once it's been damaged. A failure to acknowledge past harms is a super common thing, and you're unable to move forward if you don't acknowledge past harms."
The authority on meaningful public participation and trust repair; her framework set the standard the ten-speakers-in-two-years figure is measured against. Her prior RAND study is archived and hashed in this repository.

Board members

Erika Strassburger

Board Vice Chair; Pittsburgh City Councilmember

Recorded interview · November 2025

"What we end up voting on is fairly uncontroversial... Much like at city council, the majority of resolutions that we vote on are uncontroversial, and there wouldn't be much reason for discussion."
"We could be better about daylighting some of the hard discussions we have internally... I'll own that, and we should all own that."
The board's own account of why its votes are unanimous: executive sessions, the routine nature of most resolutions, and a candid acknowledgment that the hard discussion happens off the public record.

The utility, on the record

Will Pickering

Chief Executive Officer, Pittsburgh Water

Written response to questions

"[Unanimity reflects] consistent education, tenured board members who understand ongoing projects, and informational discussions prior to the public meetings."
"I encourage viewing the video recording of the full board meetings, as you will see open discussion at a higher level and for a longer period than our peers."
The utility's own explanation of the unanimity, given the full right of reply. Every adverse figure in the story was put to the utility before publication; the responses are preserved.

Every quotation above was verified character-by-character against the recording or written response in the investigation's 22-section master fact-check. Interviews were conducted by Brian Nuckols for Public Source, October–November 2025. Two board members, an outside expert on water governance, a media-law attorney, two community organizers, and the utility's chief executive is a wider on-record base than most local accountability stories carry, and it is the reason the finding survived the utility's response rather than being softened by it. The linked transcripts are hosted here; the linked recordings are the original audio, held on Google Drive and available to anyone with access. Sciulli and Pickering responded in writing; those responses are preserved in the fact-check record. Transcripts for Aly Shaw and Emily Ruth are held as reporter files.
